About

Content taught me what agents are missing.

I’ve spent 15+ years leading technical content organizations for Silicon Valley companies — building the documentation, knowledge systems, and content architecture that products depend on. Most recently that meant taking an enterprise content operation through an AI-native transformation: restructuring how content is authored, governed, and delivered so that agents, not just people, can consume it well.

That work surfaced a pattern. At every point where an AI agent touches the enterprise — the content it ingests, the customer intelligence it’s handed, the artifacts it produces — the connective architecture is missing: no unified governance over what agents consume, no verifiable record of what they’re told, no evaluation of what they emit that accounts for how flaws travel. I filed three provisional patents on those three gaps. The briefs on this site are the plain-English versions; full specifications are available on request.

Two things about how to read them. First, they’re a portfolio, not a product pitch: I file these as demonstrations of how I think about agent-era systems, and the conversations I’m looking for are about building — product, strategy, and knowledge-architecture roles at companies working on agents, evaluation, and the content that feeds them — not about licensing. Second, they’re snapshots of an argument still in motion. The agent landscape moves monthly, and each of these briefs has already been revised by what the market did next; I consider that a feature of the practice, not a bug in the documents.

If any of this maps to a problem your team is working on, I’d welcome the conversation: rjslogar@gmail.com · LinkedIn.
